Pediatric Care Electronic Tattoos

Remember when you were a kid and got a fun cast or sticker when you when to the doctor? What if that feeling could be reproduced digitally to make hospital stays more fun for kids and serve as a useful way for medical professionals to monitor and communicate with their patients.

I was lucky enough to be part of a team of 2 other students at the MIT Media Lab in a workshop led by Cindy Kao, the inventor of DuoSkin. Throughout the workshop we learned the DuoSkin fabrication process of fabricating conductive tattoos that are ready to be designed into integrated, interactive electronics.

We wanted to create a fun temporary tattoo for children to communicate and log their pain readings with their medical providers.

The overall system involves a tattoo, GUI, and web server. The tattoo, even though is in conductive with embedded electronics, is applied just like a traditional temporary tattoo. In the hospital setting they’d probably last about a week.

My team designed a bear tattoo, with two buttons. Another member of my team designed the bear and button icons. This was the design, as done by Josh Woodward.

Overall, the user can press down on the part of the bear where they are in pain; the longer they press the higher the pain reading. They also have two buttons they can press; the bell, which calls the doctor, and the report button which starts the recording process. An LED confirms when these buttons are pressed or when a doctor is going to come visit them.

This is what the tattoo looked like when it was fabricated.

We put it on a mannequin arm because it was easier for debugging. The wires from the tattoo go around the arm where there is a small micro controller and Bluetooth chip for logic and a small 3V battery. The whole thing is about 2 by 3 inches and is extremely light and flat and easy to mount on the skin by using medical or athletic tape.

We integrated the circuit inside the bear to be capacitive touch logic. This logic was communicated via bluetooth to the software where the pain readings were logged and stored in an easy to use web server.

Here’s a GIF of the tattoo in action!

Inside the software the doctors can see their patients and if the patient is requesting a doctor, they get a notification on the home screen.

Each patient has a profile with real time data recordings and is also connected to the web server where the doctor can view more detailed reports of the patient.

I was personally responsible for the capacitive sensing logic and calibration, communication with the software and web server, and design of the GUI. My other teammate designed the tattoo and fabricated it. Another teammate configured the Bluetooth with the software and built the web server.

All the code is on GitHub. Overall this was an awesomely fun project and I was so happy to be involved.
